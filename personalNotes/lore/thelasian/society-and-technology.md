# Thelasian Society and Technology

> DM Eyes Only. Details for running Thelasian encounters, describing ruins, and understanding what players are walking through.

---

## THE FOUNDING PRINCIPLE AND WHAT IT BUILT

The Thelasians did not merely avoid worshipping gods — they organized their entire civilization around the institutional memory that divine dependence is catastrophically dangerous. This was not passive. It was the active, daily project of a society built by six people who had served a god and watched him try to end everything.

The result was a civilization that solved every problem mortals normally solve with prayer using mundane ingenuity instead:

| Problem | Normal Solution | Thelasian Solution |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| Light in darkness | Divine fire, blessed torches | Bioluminescent glowworm cultivation |
| Healing | Clerical prayer, divine herbs | Mineral chemistry, surgical technique, cleansing pools |
| Labour | Divine servants, blessing-enhanced workers | Golem technology (Artificer-designed constructs) |
| Communication between cities | Divine messengers, prayer | Physical portal network (arcane engineering, not prayer) |
| Food underground | Divine growth miracles | Circle of Spores fungal cultivation, careful crop rotation |
| Water purification | Blessed water | Mineral filtration engineering |

They were proud of this. The pride was genuine and also defensive — it was the pride of people who needed to believe they had made the right choice, who measured their rightness by what they built without gods.

## THE CITIES

### Vethras (Capital)
The largest and oldest city, built into a massive cavern system approximately 200 meters below the swamp surface. Named from the dwarven word for "shelter found."

At peak population (~40,000), Vethras contained:
- **The portal hub** — the central node of Maerath's network, maintained by successive Thread-Keepers. Twelve active portals at peak, connecting to the other cities and four surface locations.
- **The Academy** — the Thelasians' centre of learning. No magical theory was taught here; the curriculum was engineering, medicine, history, and craft. Wick Tallow's golem technology was developed in the Academy's workshops. Morden Far's archive was housed here.
- **The Great Cleansing Pools** — Senne Ashvale's design, expanded across three centuries into an extensive public health infrastructure. Every resident of Vethras passed through a cleansing pool at least weekly.
- **The Administrative District** — Orra Dusk's governance model in physical form. Every governing decision for Vethras's entire history was recorded and stored in a public archive adjacent to the Assembly Hall.
- **Residential districts** — tiered into the cavern walls, Thael Ironroot's layered design. The topmost residences were highest status (closest to the main passages, most air circulation); the lowest were agricultural workers and golem-maintenance technicians.

### Delrath (The Mining City)
Built deeper east, through a passage Thael Ironroot identified in Year -320. Primarily focused on metalworking — the copper and iron for golem construction came from Delrath. The city had a reputation for practicality to the point of bluntness; Delrath administrators were known for refusing any meeting agenda that did not include a materials summary.

Delrath sealed its passages to Vethras in Year -300, during the resource crisis. This is the source of significant historical guilt in Thelasian records — Vethras archivists wrote extensively about the sealing, less about their own role in the negotiations that preceded it.

### Ossevane (The Agricultural City)
Built near a system of underground rivers in Year -340. Brenn Castwater founded it and his descendants maintained it as the civilization's primary food source. At peak it supplied 40% of Thelasian caloric needs through fungal cultivation and underground river farming.

Ossevane lost its underground river system to geological shift around Year -280 — a delayed consequence of the Veiled Peaks raising. The agricultural output collapsed. The city tried to adapt for 30 years before its population dispersed to Vethras.

## THELASIAN TECHNOLOGY

### Bioluminescent Glowworm Cultivation

The light source of the entire civilization. A species of cave glowworm already present in the founding cavern when the Six arrived — Brenn Castwater spent six months studying them before he understood how to cultivate them at scale.

**How it works:** Glowworms require moisture, specific fungal food sources, and darkness. They produce a cold blue-white light from a bioluminescent organ in their thorax. Individual worms produce minimal light, but colonies of thousands lining tunnel walls and ceiling niches produce consistent, even illumination. The light is cold — it does not warm the air or affect temperature.

**Colour and quality:** The glow is blue-white, slightly eerie, beautiful in large spaces. It creates no shadows in corners — the distributed sources fill the space evenly. It does not flicker. Players entering the ruins will describe it as otherworldly and peaceful, which it is. The Thelasians thought of it as normal.

**Maintenance requirements:** Spore rotation every three months, moisture calibration by humidity, cleaning of the fungal substrate. When the civilization collapsed and maintenance stopped, the glowworms died in some areas and spread uncontrolled in others. In Year 5: active in the town level and cleansing pool areas (Velthara's servants have been inadvertently maintaining them); dead in the deeper archive level; faintly active in patches near the deepest ley line convergence.

**The mushroom hazard:** Certain fungal species in the ruins — a byproduct of uncontrolled cultivation after the collapse — release spore clouds when disturbed. These cause temporary blindness (1d4 rounds) and are visually indistinguishable from safe fungi without a DC 14 Nature check. Brenn Castwater's descendants knew how to manage them. The players do not.

### The Cleansing Pools

Senne Ashvale's masterwork. Underground mineral water, filtered through a specific sequence of mineral deposits (limestone, kaolin, trace iron), collecting in carved stone basins. The mineral filtration removes pathogens, adds trace minerals with genuine healing properties, and produces a faint warm sensation on skin contact.

**The inscription's practical meaning:** *"All who go further must be clean of body and mind / Step carefully... impurity will be punished."* The "punishment" for not cleansing was historically disease — people who bypassed the pool in the early years got sick. The spiritual framing came later. The physical reality remained: cleansed travelers got sick less, healed faster, and (per Senne's records) showed measurably better wound recovery.

**Game mechanics:** Players who fully submerge in a functioning cleansing pool gain the benefits of a short rest plus advantage on Constitution saving throws against disease for 24 hours. No magic involved. Just good mineral chemistry.

**The access ritual:** *"Come as you are and be refused / Come as you were and enter true."* This inscription is at the portal entrance, not the pool. Its meaning: the portal only opens to someone who has performed the cleansing ritual first — the original Thelasian requirement, baked into the portal activation sequence. You must wash your hands and approach the entrance with clean intent. A person actively intending harm to the civilization cannot activate it. [[Velthara]]'s servants can enter because Velthara has no hostile intent toward the ruins — she wants them intact as her lair.

### Golem Technology

Wick Tallow's creation, built on Thael Ironroot's foundation prototype. Three fully developed classes, one incomplete:

**Janitor-class:** The most common golem in the ruins. Stone body, approximately human-sized, with simplified articulated arms. Programming (encoded in copper rune-plates in the torso) handles a defined territory of corridors, a cleaning implement, and a disposal procedure. The janitor golem players encounter in the town level is Janitor Unit 7 of the original Vethras residential district. It has been sweeping the same 40-meter corridor for 350 years. It will not stop for players unless they physically block it for more than 10 seconds, at which point it will wait patiently, then resume.

**Porter-class:** Larger, stronger, designed for cargo transport between districts. Most have wound down — the portal energy they ran on stopped replenishing. A few in areas of residual ley-line activity still move sluggishly, following old transport routes. Encountering one on a path is not dangerous but is startling.

**Record-class:** Smallest and most delicate. Human-torso sized, with fine articulated fingers. Designed for filing, retrieving, and copying documents in the archive. All wound down in the archive level, where the ley-line energy does not reach. They sit at desks in the archive, mid-task, as if they paused for a moment and never started again.

**Guardian-class (incomplete):** Wick Tallow's last project, never finished. The prototype sits in his workshop on Level 3. It is larger than the other classes — designed for defense. The rune-plates for locomotion are carved and installed. The judgment-logic rune-plates (determining what constitutes a threat, how to respond) are blank. Wick could not solve the judgment problem before he died. A note pinned to its chest reads: *"Not ready. Still working out the judgment logic — how it decides what counts as a threat. Will return to this."*

An Artificer character who spends a week with Wick's blueprints and a successful DC 20 Arcana check could complete the guardian-class. It would become a CR 5 construct with 75 HP, AC 17, and two attacks (+6, 2d8+4 bludgeoning), but with the judgment logic unresolved, it would need explicit daily instructions about who counts as a threat. Without instructions it defaults to attacking anything that moves in its patrol zone.

### The Portal Network

[[Maerath]]'s threads, physically manifested as carved stone doorframes with specific ley-line anchoring at each site. The Thread-Keeper maintained the network by performing regular calibration on each anchor — essentially keeping the connection precise enough to traverse safely. An imprecisely maintained portal could deposit travelers off-target by anywhere from three feet to a mile.

**In Year 5:** The network is in residual operation. Portals are unreliable — they open when Maerath stirs in his sleep, close when he settles. The one near the swamp opened on its own in Year 4, allowing Dr. Clair's excavation to begin. The portals to Delrath and Ossevane last opened in approximately Year -240 before the receiving anchors degraded. Those cities are not accessible by portal. The portal to Aeldris Vorn's tower in the [[Veiled Peaks]] — if players find it — would require recalibration. Someone with Maerath's symbol and an understanding of ley-line anchoring could attempt it (DC 22 Arcana check, with advantage if they have read Aeldris Vorn's research notes).

## THELASIAN CULTURE

### The Purity Doctrine

The cleansing pool requirement was practical in origin and became philosophical over centuries. Thelasian culture placed extraordinary value on what they called "clean intent" — the idea that actions taken from clear, honest motivation produce better outcomes than actions taken from mixed or hidden ones. This was not naïve: they understood that people have mixed motivations. The doctrine was an aspiration and a governance principle. Civic leaders were expected to publicly state their reasoning. Records were kept. Revisionism was considered a serious transgression.

The irony that their civilization was founded by people fleeing a morally complex situation they never fully confronted was noted by later Thelasian scholars. Orra Dusk's governance records contain several passages that read, in retrospect, as indirect self-criticism.

### The Anti-Divine Stance

The Thelasians did not actively hate gods — they were careful about this. Active hostility toward gods was considered impractical: gods exist, they have power, hatred accomplishes nothing useful. What the Thelasians maintained was a deliberate policy of non-engagement. They used Maerath's portals without praying to Maerath. They were grateful without being supplicants. They found this distinction important.

In practice, the distinction blurred over generations. By Year -250, most Thelasians had forgotten the founding reason for the anti-divine stance. They maintained it as cultural tradition — gods are unpredictable, better not to involve them — without knowing it originated in direct experience of what happened when a god was involved.

Morden Far's records preserve the reason. His descendants copied the records without understanding them.

### Death Practices

The Thelasians cremated their dead and returned the ash to the fungal cultivation substrate — a biological cycle that Brenn Castwater designed and the civilization adopted universally. No monuments, no marked graves. The dead fed the food that fed the living. This was considered beautiful by Thelasians and unsettling by the few surface visitors who learned of it.

There are no Thelasian burial sites in the ruins. There are no bones. The last generation cremated their dead and deposited the ash in the cultivation beds, then sealed the city. Players looking for remains will find nothing.

### Language and Script

Morden Far's invented alphabet. Precise, angular, originally adapted from academic notation — letters that were designed to be written quickly and read precisely, not elegantly. The script appears throughout the ruins on everything: wall inscriptions, golem programming plates, archive documents, administrative records, the cleansing pool instructions, the entrance stone.

A player with the Linguist feat or proficiency in Calligrapher's Supplies can attempt to read Thelasian script with a DC 16 Intelligence check, with advantage if they have spent at least an hour studying it in context. A success yields the general meaning; a failure by 5 or more yields a plausible-sounding but incorrect reading.

The two inscriptions players have already encountered use simplified formal Thelasian — the equivalent of public signage, written for easy reading. The archive documents use academic Thelasian, significantly harder. Morden Far's personal notes use a shorthand that requires his codex (also in the archive) to decipher.

## WHAT THE RUINS SMELL LIKE

Players ask this. Thelasian ruins have a specific sensory profile:

- **Upper levels (threshold and town):** Cool, mineral-clean, faint organic sweetness from the glowworm cultivation medium. Not unpleasant. Like clean stone and something biological that isn't threatening.
- **Archive level:** Drier. Old parchment, mineral dust, the faint metallic smell of copper-plated golem rune-plates. A deep stillness.
- **Velthara's levels:** The mineral smell fades. Something sweeter and wrong underneath — [[Blood Root]] at too-high a concentration, the way medicine smells when the dosage is too high. Beneath that: a faint heat-smell that has no name, coming from the crimson webs.
- **Deepest level:** Cold. Very still air. The mineral smell returns, but fainter, and underneath it something vast and dormant that no one can quite identify. Like standing in a room where something important is sleeping.
