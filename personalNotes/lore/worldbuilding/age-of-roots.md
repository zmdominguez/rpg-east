## THE WORLD BEFORE THORHAVEN

Thornhaven sits in a valley that was unremarkable for most of recorded history. No battle was fought here. No empire rose here. The settlers who arrived in Year 0 believed they had found an unusually fertile, resource-rich frontier. They were right. They did not understand why.

The valley was chosen. Every resource the settlers found — the clean water, the wild game, the rich soil, the Blood root in the hills — was positioned there by design. The design was ancient. But the designer was not powerful when the story began. He became powerful because of it.

That is the end of the story. The beginning is older, and worse.

## THE GODS OF THE AGE OF ROOTS

### Gorethis the Blooming — Later Called the Verdant Tyrant

**Domain:** Growth, cultivation, harvest, the fertility of living things  
**Symbol:** A vine-wrapped golden sun  
**Epithets:** The Blooming Lord, the Green Hand, (later) the Tyrant Below

Gorethis was once among the most beloved gods. Agricultural civilizations prayed to him at planting and harvest. He was warm, generous in aspect, and genuinely cared for the creatures who tended the land. His priests healed with herbs. His festivals were the loudest of the calendar.

His corruption was not sudden. It arrived the way vines grow — quietly, over years, until the wall is already covered.

He discovered it by accident: a fragment of his divine essence, spilled into a root-garden during a ritual blessing, was returned to him during harvest amplified tenfold. The root had consumed his power, held it through its growth cycle, and given it back multiplied. He called this the **Verdant Cycle**.

He spent fifty years refining the process before telling anyone. He concluded that with enough Blood root, cultivated with focused divine intent, he could accumulate more power than all other gods combined. He told himself this was *for* the world — that he would become the All-God, the single benevolent shepherd of all creation, ending the petty conflicts between divine domains. He believed this completely. He was not lying to himself. He was wrong about what he wanted, which is more dangerous than a lie.

**Status — Current Day:** Gorethis is not dead. He was never destroyed. He was *dispersed* — his essence broken into fragments and sealed within every Blood root plant in the world. The amplification property that makes Blood root remarkable is not a feature of the plant. It is Gorethis, still responding to intent, still cycling. He is dormant, but the fragments remember. If enough Blood root is cultivated under the complete Verdant Cycle ritual, the fragments will reassemble. He will wake.

**The fragments feel this:** When Blood root is used for healing, the fragment inside experiences satisfaction. When used for dark intent, the fragment surges with recognition. This is why Blood root amplifies whatever it is fed — it is not neutral. It has a will. A sleeping one.

### Maerath the Keeper of Threads

**Domain:** Fate, memory, connections, portals  
**Symbol:** A spider web with a single golden thread at its center  
**Epithets:** The Thread-Keeper, the Weaver of What-Was (note: Velthara's title "The Crimson Weaver" was given by those who did not know its origin)

Maerath was the god of invisible connections — the threads between past and future, the bridges between places, the filaments of memory that tie a person to who they were. The Thelasian civilization worshipped him above all others, because his portals made their underground trade network possible.

He was the primary architect of the response to Gorethis. Where others reacted with alarm, Maerath mapped — traced every thread, modeled every consequence, calculated every outcome. When the scope of Gorethis's plan became clear, Maerath spent ten years running projections. He concluded:
- Direct confrontation would fail (Gorethis could weaponize the accumulation against attackers)
- Destroying the Blood root was impossible (it had spread too far, and destroying it would disperse Gorethis's essence catastrophically)
- The only solution was a predator — something that could consume Blood root faster than Gorethis could cycle it, not susceptible to his influence, impossible to corrupt

He created Velthara.

**The Creation of Velthara:**
Maerath wove her over five years. He took threads of pure divine will — not a mortal soul, not an animal transformed, but something genuinely new — and shaped them into a living predator. He gave her:
- A hunger that could never be satisfied, specifically attuned to Blood root
- The ability to consume divine essence without being destroyed by it
- A body armored against Gorethis's influence
- A mind capable of strategy, adaptation, and long-term planning — she would need all of it

What he did not give her: memory of her origin. Memory of why she existed. Memory of Gorethis. Memory of herself as something *made*.

He was afraid that if she knew she was created as a weapon, she would ask questions. Questions would lead to hesitation. Hesitation would give Gorethis time to adapt. So he made her blank — instinct and hunger and intelligence with no history.

He told himself this was temporary. Once Gorethis was sealed, he would restore her memories. He would tell her what she was. He would give her the choice of what to become.

He fell asleep before he could do this. He has been sleeping for six hundred years.

**Maerath's symbol was a spider web.** He created Velthara as a spider. She is, literally, his sigil made flesh and given hunger. Every depiction of Maerath in old Thelasian art shows a web. Players who find these carvings in the Thelasian ruins, who then encounter Velthara, may eventually understand what they mean.

**Status — Current Day:** Maerath is stirring. The portal that opened in Year 4 during Dr. Clair's excavation was not a coincidence — it opened because Maerath, deep in his divine sleep, felt Velthara moving above him, and some fragment of ancient instinct caused the portal network to activate. As Velthara grows more powerful (consuming Blood root, accumulating Gorethis's essence), the disturbance above Maerath's sleeping place grows louder. He is waking slowly. He does not yet know what Year it is, what Velthara has become, or what has happened to Gorethis.

When he wakes fully, the campaign enters a new phase.

### Velenth the Storm-Singer

**Domain:** Storms, dramatic weather, the chaos of the sky  
**Status:** Dead

Velenth was a minor deity — passionate, loud, beloved by sailors and performers. He joined Maerath in the first direct confrontation with Gorethis not because he had a strategic role but because he was the sort of god who showed up when a fight needed witnesses.

Gorethis killed him. He used the Verdant Cycle energy to grow Blood root vines through the storm-channels that carried Velenth's divine presence. The vines smothered the sky. Velenth's essence scattered as a three-month thunderstorm that killed crops across the known world.

He is not remembered. There are no temples to him. There is no mythology, no prayer, no mention in any surviving text. Gods do not eulogize other gods — they simply stop appearing in the stories.

This is what death looks like for a deity in this world. Not a monument. Silence.

*The players may find, in very old ruins, carvings of a figure with lightning in his hands. No name. No context. Just a symbol that no one alive can explain.*

### Scarsith — A Minor God in an Age of Greater Powers

**Domain:** Survival, scarcity, perseverance  
**Status during the Age of Roots:** Minor deity — few temples, thin following, limited power  
**Role in the Age of Roots:** The one who noticed. Nothing more.

At the time of the War of Thorns, Scarsith was small. His worship was scattered — prisoners, refugees, the desperately sick, sailors in bad weather who had already prayed to Velenth and gotten no answer. He had no grand temples. No armies of the faithful. He was the god of people who were out of better options.

What he had, in place of power, was sensitivity. Scarsith's entire domain is built on recognizing imbalance — the difference between real scarcity and false abundance. When Gorethis's Verdant Cycle began distorting the divine realm, a major god might have felt a vague warmth, a pleasant surplus, and moved on. Scarsith felt it like a wrong note in a familiar song. *Something is eating without being seen.*

He brought his evidence to Maerath. Laid it out carefully, precisely, because he knew no one would take seriously a minor god making claims about a major one without extraordinary proof. Maerath listened. Spent ten years confirming it.

That was Scarsith's contribution to the Age of Roots. He was not part of the confrontation — he was not powerful enough for that to mean anything. He watched from the margins as Maerath built Velthara, as the Long Consumption played out, as Maeris died, as Aeldris raised the mountains.

He watched the aftermath too. And the aftermath is what made him.

**How Scarsith grew:**
- Velenth's death caused a three-month storm and a famine across the known world. The starving and desperate prayed for survival. Scarsith answered every prayer he could. He grew.
- The Thelasian civilization's slow collapse over two centuries created waves of displaced, hungry people who had no patron deity, no community structure, no hope. They prayed to whatever was listening. Scarsith was always listening. He grew.
- The Jackal War — decades of grinding conflict producing refugees — fed him further. By its end he had more temples than he had had in the entire previous millennium.
- The god of scarcity flourished in an age shaped by scarcity. He did not cause the suffering. He simply outlasted every deity who could not help the people who were drowning in it.

By Year 0 he was no longer minor. And he had a plan.

**Thorhaven as Scarsith's Later Project:**
Centuries after the Age of Roots, Scarsith finally had the power and the reach to execute what he had understood from the beginning: the solution to Gorethis was not force, but fragmentation. Keep Blood root constantly harvested and consumed by struggling, desperate mortals. Scattered mundane use prevents the divine accumulation required to wake Gorethis.

So he guided the right survivors to the right valley. He planted identical dreams in scattered, homeless people: *a valley east, rich with medicine in the mountains, clean water from old channels, a place to start again.* Seven families responded. They did not know each other. They arrived in the valley within weeks of one another and called it Thornhaven.

The Church of Scarsith was the first permanent structure built in Thornhaven — before houses, before the Fat Pig, before the walls. This was not tradition. It was infrastructure.

**The dark irony Scarsith carries:** He grew powerful because the Age of Roots left such catastrophic suffering in its wake. He benefited from every calamity Gorethis caused and every sacrifice that sealed him. He did not cause any of it. He would not have chosen it. But he is what he is because of it, and he knows it, and he does not allow himself to forget.

**What Scarsith knows now (Year 5):** Everything. He has been watching with focused attention. He knows Velthara has woken. He knows she is using Blood root to accumulate divine power — doing exactly what Gorethis did, in the same place, for the same reason she was made to prevent. He also knows that if Velthara is killed, Gorethis's fragments inside her will be released at once. He is calculating. His primary agent in Thornhaven — Nell — has been compromised by Velthara. He is routing through less obvious channels.

He will reach out when the moment is right. Through a dream, a rat that follows a player with suspicious intelligence, or a Scarsith priest who receives a message they cannot explain: *"Tell them: the Tyrant is not below. He is inside the Weaver. Do not cut her thread until his fragments are scattered. Find the tower in the peaks."*

## MAERIS THORNWEAVE — THE ROOTMOTHER

**Full Name:** Maeris Thornweave  
**Title:** The Rootmother (posthumous, given by the Owl Cult descendants)  
**Status:** Dead — but not gone

Maeris was a mortal woman who served Gorethis for thirty years as the head of his druidic circle, the **Circle of the Verdant Root**. She believed she was healing the land.

Gorethis had told her the truth, approximately. The Verdant Cycle *was* returning divine energy to the soil. He simply omitted that it was being extracted from the divine realm to return amplified to him, and that the net effect was accumulating power toward the single goal of his ascension.

When Maerath revealed the truth to her, she did not speak for three days. Then she went back to the garden.

She had twelve druids under her. She gathered them and told them what they had been building. Six left immediately — shame, fear, or self-preservation. She did not stop them. Six stayed.

She walked to the center of the garden, to the ancient stone where Gorethis had first planted his own divine fragment, and she opened herself. She pulled every accumulation of divine essence in the garden through her own body — broke the cycle by becoming the circuit breaker. She absorbed what would have taken Velthara another decade to consume.

It destroyed her. A mortal body receiving that much divine power is like a bucket receiving the ocean.

She had time — minutes — to make one final choice. She spent them binding her dying life force to the roots. Not to be preserved, not to persist, but to continue the work: holding the fragments, guarding the accumulation point, keeping the last and largest concentration of Gorethis's essence sealed in the earth.

She has been doing this for six hundred years.

**What Maeris knows:** She saw everything. She saw Velthara consume the garden. She saw Aeldris raise the mountain. She saw the Owl Cult forget. She saw Pipe Ashford arrive, exhausted and grieving, and begin to understand the carvings. She has been trying to communicate with Pipe for three years through the behavior of the vines — the direction they grow, which plants bloom and which wither, where the Root Guardian positions itself.

Pipe thinks she is interpreting the garden's moods. She is interpreting Maeris.

**What Maeris wants Pipe to know:** Gorethis is not fully sealed. The fragments inside Velthara are stirring as she accumulates more power. Velthara is becoming the new Verdant Cycle — not intentionally, but the mechanism is identical. And if Velthara is killed before the fragments inside her are dispersed safely, Gorethis converges from her corpse.

The garden is dying — not from neglect but from the fragments inside it weakening as the seal degrades. The Root Guardian is Maeris's last sustained effort. When it finally falls, the seal breaks.

**The Root Guardian:** Not a creature. An animated manifestation of Maeris's remaining will, using the root system as a body. It is not hostile to those who approach with genuine, protective intent. It is hostile to anything that tries to harvest or damage the garden. If the players fight it and win, Maeris's last defense is gone.

## THE CIRCLE OF THE VERDANT ROOT — THE SIX AND THE SIX

**The Six Who Fled:**
Druids who abandoned Maeris when the truth was revealed. They traveled south and east, eventually going underground — literally. They founded the **Thelasian civilization** in the deep places, using their agricultural knowledge to grow food without sunlight (bioluminescent fungal cultivation, the cleansing pools for purification). Their founding principle, passed down through generations, was that divine power is dangerous. They built a sophisticated mundane civilization specifically because they refused to rely on gods.

The Thelasian ruins are the remnants of this civilization. The descendants of these six druids built something extraordinary — and then watched it slowly fail when Aeldris sealed the portals.

**The Six Who Stayed:**
After Maeris's death, Maerath transformed these six druids into owls to protect them from Gorethis's remaining divine followers, who were hunting everyone connected to the garden. The transformation was called the **Owlmark**. It was meant to last months, perhaps a year, until Maerath could dissolve the threat.

Maerath fell asleep two years later. The Owlmark passed down through bloodlines. The original six never transformed back. Their children were born with the curse dormant, activating under stress. Their grandchildren simply... were this way.

Over generations, the Owl Cult lost the original context entirely. They know:
- They are bound to the forest and the peaks
- The garden is sacred
- Humans are intruders to be driven off
- Transformation is normal and not a curse but a gift

They do not know:
- Why they transform
- That the Owlmark was cast by a god to protect, not punish
- That the garden they guard is a god's prison
- That the moment they *remember*, the transformation can be broken

**Gravy, the eldest Owl Cult druid**, was transformed so long ago that he no longer transforms back. He is permanently an owl. He alone carries dim memory-fragments that do not make sense to him — a woman's voice, the smell of vines, a moment of terrible choice. He thinks they are ancestral dreams. They are Maeris.

## AELDRIS VORN — THE THREAD-KEEPER

**Full Name:** Aeldris Vorn  
**Title:** Thread-Keeper of Maerath  
**Age at the time of events:** Approximately 60 years old  
**Status:** Dead — died alone, approximately twenty years after the sealing

Aeldris Vorn was the greatest living arcanist of the Thelasian civilization. His specialization was ley line manipulation — the rerouting of the invisible power-channels that run through the earth like veins. The Thelasians used these ley lines to power their portal network, their golems, their bioluminescent lighting. Aeldris maintained all of it.

He had served Maerath, in the Thelasian fashion, not as a worshipper but as a professional — the way a harbor master serves the sea. Maerath's portals required mortal maintenance. Aeldris was the maintenance.

When Maerath called on him to aid in the sealing of Gorethis, Aeldris understood immediately what it would cost. He had run the calculations before Maerath finished explaining. Sealing the portals would isolate the Thelasian civilization from the trade networks they depended on. Without those networks, they would decline. Within 200 years, they would collapse.

He agreed immediately.

His recorded reasoning, from the journal now buried in the deepest chamber of the Thelasian ruins:

> *"I have lived sixty years in a civilization built on the shame of six druids who chose themselves over the world. I will not be the seventh."*

**What Aeldris did:**

**1. The Water Channels** — Before raising the mountains, Aeldris rerouted the underground ley lines flowing beneath the garden valley to create permanent water channels. These channels still flow today — they are what Venn maintains in the Veiled Peaks, what irrigates Thornhaven's farmland. They were designed to last without maintenance. They have. This was Aeldris ensuring that whatever settlement eventually grew below the mountain would have reason to stay — and reason to keep harvesting Blood root from the lower slopes.

**2. The Raising of the Veiled Peaks** — Using every accumulated unit of energy in the Thelasian portal network discharged at once through a single ley line convergence point, Aeldris caused a geological event that lifted the earth beneath the Rootmother's Garden. The mountains grew in three days. What had been a highland valley became a peak. The garden was raised out of reach, hidden beneath crags and snowfall. This used all the stored energy in the network and permanently damaged the ley line architecture — portals would work on residual flow for centuries but could never again be reliably opened at will.

**3. The Memory Thread** — The most delicate work. Maerath had created Velthara without memories and intended this to remain until he could safely restore them. But before he fell asleep, Maerath asked Aeldris to weave a secondary thread — a reinforcing suppression around the gap where Velthara's origin should be. Something that would hold even if Maerath slept, that would prevent her from reconstructing the truth through inference alone. Aeldris wove it through her web-nature, as thin as gossamer, as strong as iron. It has held for six hundred years. It is degrading now. Velthara occasionally has moments of — not memory, but *resonance*. A feeling that she has done this before. That she was made for this.

**4. The Journal** — Before sealing himself out of his own civilization, Aeldris wrote everything down. Names. Dates. The Verdant Cycle mechanics. The reason for his choices. He sealed it in the deepest chamber beneath the Thelasian ruins in a locked box of ley-warded stone that could only be opened by someone who had already found the portal Maerath built as an entrance key. He did this because he believed, eventually, someone would need to know.

**After the sealing:** Aeldris could not portal home. He walked. He spent twenty years moving south through mountains, surviving on Blood root ironically, gradually making his way toward warmer lands. He died somewhere north of what would eventually become Thornhaven. His tower — built into the Veiled Peaks during his earlier work on the water channels — still stands. It is above the current snow line, difficult to access, unsealed. His real research notes are there, not in the ruins. The journal is a confession. The tower is a laboratory. The tower contains the full mechanics of the Verdant Cycle and — critically — the information needed to perform a *controlled* dispersal, the one thing that could scatter Gorethis's fragments inside Velthara safely, without convergence.

## THE FULL CHAIN — HOW EVERYTHING CONNECTS

**Blood root is Gorethis.** Every plant in the hills, every poultice Naven dispenses, every crop in the peaks — each is a fragment of a sealed deity still faintly dreaming. The amplification property is not botanical. It is divine will responding to intent.

**Velthara is Maerath's creation.** She was built to consume Gorethis. She did her job. She is still doing her job — she just doesn't know why, or that there is a limit. She has been eating divinity for six hundred years and has become, slowly, divine herself. She is doing exactly what Gorethis did. The irony is complete.

**Killing Velthara releases Gorethis.** All of the divine essence she has consumed over six centuries — Gorethis's fragments — will converge at the moment of her death. He will not wake gradually. He will wake all at once, with a body made of six centuries of accumulated grievance. This is the trap. The players cannot simply kill the villain.

**The Owl Cult are guardians who forgot.** Their "curse" is Maerath's protective spell. Gravy's permanent owl form is what happens when the original caster sleeps for six hundred years — the temporary becomes permanent. If Maerath is woken and informed, he could reverse it. If the Owl Cult learns the truth, the Owlmark can be broken by choice.

**Thornhaven is a weapon.** A slow weapon. A scarcity engine designed to fragment Blood root usage and prevent divine accumulation. It is working — or was, until Velthara started gathering it all.

**Scarsith is not a hero.** He is not a villain either. He is a survivor who grew powerful because the Age of Roots left catastrophic suffering in its wake — and because he answered those prayers when no one else would. He did not cause the tragedy. He could not have stopped it. But he is what he is because of it, and Thornhaven is his long, slow, aching attempt to make something useful out of that truth.

**Pipe Ashford is the most important mortal alive.** She has been in the Rootmother's Garden for three years receiving Maeris's wordless warnings. She understands more of this than any living person except possibly Scarsith. She doesn't know she understands — she thinks she has learned about herbs.

**Aeldris's tower exists.** It is in the Veiled Peaks, above the snow line. It contains the full mechanics of the Verdant Cycle and — critically — the method for controlled dispersal of divine fragments. This is the key to the endgame: you cannot kill Velthara without releasing Gorethis, but you *can* perform a controlled dispersal first, scatter the fragments safely, and then kill her. The tower has the instructions.

**Maerath waking is not necessarily bad.** He created this mess. He can fix parts of it. He can reverse the Owlmark, restore Velthara's memories (which would devastate her — she may become an unexpected ally), and access the portal network properly. But waking a sleeping god requires someone who knows how, and Maerath's worshippers are six centuries gone.

**Eman's green eyes.** Aasimar divine heritage typically traces to a specific deity. The green eyes — the color of new growth, of vine and leaf — are not a common Aasimar color. They are Gorethis's color. Eman may be a divine vessel shaped by Gorethis's lingering influence. She does not know this. Neither does Scarsith, though he suspects it. This could mean: Eman is drawn to Blood root instinctively. Eman's survival instincts are unnaturally sharp near Blood root. And — the darkest possibility — Eman could become a host for Gorethis's convergence if the right conditions are met.

## SCARSITH'S CURRENT PROBLEM

Scarsith has been running a containment operation for three centuries. It is failing.

His scarcity engine (Thornhaven) is still functioning — the town's Blood root consumption is still fragmenting the accumulation. But Velthara is now extracting and consuming Blood root faster than Thornhaven can deplete it. The math is changing.

Nell, his primary operative in Thornhaven, is compromised by Velthara. She is being used against his plan without knowing it.

Eman, his unintentional asset, is politically powerful but also possibly a divine vessel for his enemy.

Scarsith cannot act directly. That is not his nature — he is the god of *enduring*, not the god of *intervention*. He works through the desperate, the hungry, the survivors who have no choice but to gnaw through the obstacle.

He is watching the players. He has been for some time.
