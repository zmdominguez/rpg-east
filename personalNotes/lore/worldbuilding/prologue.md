# The Epic Prologue — Aeldris Vorn's Final Journal

> **DM Note:** This is the in-world text of Aeldris Vorn's sealed journal, found in the deepest chamber of the Thelasian ruins. It is written as a confession and a warning. The players will find it after unlocking the warded stone box (portal-key required). Read it aloud at the table when they do. It is the campaign's true prologue — the players receive it mid-campaign, after they've already been living in its consequences.

---

## HOW TO PRESENT IT

When the players open the journal:

> *The pages are made of something that is not paper — pressed root fiber, stained golden-brown, dry as bone. The ink is dark red. The writing is precise but the lines grow uneven toward the end, as if the hand holding the pen was cold, or tired. The journal has no title. It begins, as many confessions do, with the thing the writer cannot stop thinking about.*

Read the journal text. Pause between sections. Let it breathe.

---

## THE JOURNAL OF AELDRIS VORN

*Thread-Keeper of Maerath*
*Last entry — written in the forty-first year of my exile*

---

I have been walking for twenty years. I do not know how far south I have come. The mountains behind me are the ones I raised, and I cannot look at them.

Let me write this plainly, while I still can. I am old and the cold does not leave my bones the way it once did, and I have no confidence that I will find anyone living before my time here ends. So I am writing it down. I am burying it. I am trusting that whoever digs it up will have come prepared to understand.

---

**What Blood Root Is**

There was once a god called Gorethis the Blooming. He tended growth and harvest. He was beloved. He was patient. And he discovered, as patient and beloved things sometimes do, that he wanted more than he had.

He discovered that if a fragment of divine essence — a tear, a drop of blood, a sliver of true divine will — was fed into the root of a certain plant during cultivation, the plant would hold it. Would grow around it. Would return it at harvest, multiplied.

He called this the Verdant Cycle.

Every Blood Root plant you have ever touched, every poultice pressed to a wound, every bitter tea drunk against fever — each of those plants contains a fragment of Gorethis. His essence, distributed across thousands of roots, waiting. The amplification you have perhaps heard attributed to the plant is not botany. It is Gorethis, still responding. Still cycling.

He is not dead. He is sleeping. There is a difference.

---

**What Was Done**

A small god noticed first. One of the gods whose name you would not have known then — his temples were few, his faithful were desperate people who had run out of better options. He felt something wrong in the divine order the way a rat feels a change in the air before a storm. He brought what he had found to Maerath the Keeper of Threads, who had the power and the precision to do something about it.

Maerath built the solution. He wove a predator from threads of divine will — a spider of his own design, made to consume Blood Root and with it, Gorethis's essence. He created her without memory of her origin. He believed she would be safer without knowing what she was. He believed he would restore her memories once the crisis passed.

He intended to. He fell asleep before he could.

The predator's name is Velthara. You may know her by another title.

She did her work well. For over a century she consumed the garden, plant by plant, fragment by fragment, pulling Gorethis out of his own seeds. He had no body left to defend. He was spread too thin. By the time a mortal woman named Maeris broke the last cycle by absorbing it into herself and died doing it — Gorethis was a scattering. Barely a whisper in a thousand roots.

Then Maerath slept.

And Velthara, her job complete, sank into the earth and slept too.

And the small god who had noticed? He watched. He waited. He grew, as small things do when the large things fall.

---

**What I Did**

Maerath asked me to finish the work he was too tired to complete.

I raised the mountains. Three days of channeled ley energy, every portal the Thelasian civilization had built for three centuries discharged at once through a single convergence point. The earth rose. The garden was buried under new peaks. Hidden from anyone who might know what they were looking for.

I sealed the portal network to prevent Gorethis's remaining divine followers from entering the underground cities and destroying the druidic descendants who had helped stop him.

I wove a thread of forgetting into Velthara's sleeping mind. To keep her blind to her own nature until Maerath woke and could give her back what she was owed.

And I destroyed the Thelasian civilization.

Not quickly. Not intentionally. But I knew — I had run the numbers, I was the one who knew the portals best — I knew that sealing the network would cut the trade routes. That without trade routes, the deep cities would slowly starve. That it would take two hundred years, and those two hundred years would be slow and quiet and very, very sad. And I sealed the portals anyway.

I have thought about this every day for twenty years.

I was born in a civilization founded by six people who ran away from a hard choice. I chose not to be the seventh.

That is all I can say in my defense. I do not know if it is enough.

---

**What You Need to Know**

Gorethis is still in the roots.

Most of his essence is inside Velthara now — consumed and held within her, dormant. The rest remains scattered in Blood Root plants across the land.

If Velthara is killed without first scattering the fragments she carries, they will converge. He will wake. Not gradually, not weakly — all at once, in the body of whatever killed her, or nearest to her death. There is no clean ending here. There is only a careful one.

There is also this: Velthara does not know what she is. She does not know she was made. She does not know she was made *for this*. She has been sleeping and waking and sleeping again for centuries with a hunger she cannot explain, consuming the thing she was built to consume, growing strange and old and powerful without understanding any of it.

She deserves to know. I could not give it to her. Maerath was supposed to.

If Maerath can be woken, he can tell her. He can restore what was taken. What she does with that knowledge — whether she becomes your enemy or your only possible ally — I cannot say. I have never met a god waking from six centuries of sleep.

---

**Where to Find What You Need**

I built a tower in the mountains. Not the new mountains — the old rock, above the snow line, east of where the water channels split. It is still sealed. The key is architectural: the door opens to a sustained tone of D, held by any instrument, for ten seconds. I was very tired when I chose this and it was all I could think of.

Inside the tower is everything I knew about the Verdant Cycle. Including the dispersal method — the controlled way to scatter Gorethis's fragments from Velthara's body without triggering convergence. It requires preparation and precision and it cannot be done in a fight. It must be done first. Then she can be stopped.

I do not know if she is still alive. I do not know if she ever woke. I do not know if any of the druids' descendants are still protecting the garden — there were twelve of them, six who stayed and were transformed, and I watched them become birds in the last moment before I began the raising. I never saw them after.

If they are still there. If she is still there. Tell them what I could not.

---

**A Final Thing**

I have been eating Blood Root for twenty years because it is hardy and grows in cold places and I have not had much else.

Sometimes, when I eat it, I think I can feel something warm and patient and very old, looking back at me through the root. Not malevolent. Not threatening. Just — present. Waiting.

I try to remember that this was someone once. That he started with something that was almost good — the desire to tend all of creation, to be the one who ensured nothing would ever be hungry or sick or dying in a ditch, which is where I am going to die, more likely than not.

I try to remember that it doesn't matter, in the end, why he wanted what he wanted. Only what it cost.

The rat endures where the lion falls. I have always thought this was about stubbornness. I think now it is about patience. About playing a game long enough to see all the consequences arrive.

The rat wins because it is still here.

I hope you are still here.

*— Aeldris Vorn, Thread-Keeper of Maerath*
*Written in exile, somewhere south of the mountains I made*
